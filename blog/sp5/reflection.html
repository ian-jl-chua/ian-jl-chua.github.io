Reflection

The 2 things I did learn about myself are how critical I am towards myself and also how reluctant I am to ask for help....

I would end up beating myself up (mentally) and thinking about how much I suck, every time someone post a question on Discord, because seeing other peoples questions, made me realize how much I DON'T understand and how far others are ahead of me. When and if I do catch this pattern, I do remind myself that I need to be aware that I'm dyslexic and I learn things differently, furthermore I'm learning this for the first time, so it might take a while to "click".

Asking for help is also a big thing, as I am quite reluctant to do this... Not sure if this is the fear of others viewing me as stupid, or just my stubbornness with the brute force approach, or maybe a bit of both. I also feel more comfortable asking for help from people I know well, as oppose to people whom I don't know.

I can see now that values, empathy, and self-awareness has a huge role, not just in learning and programming, but in life in general as well. I do feel that values are somewhat subjective, as this varies from individual to individual based on one's upbringing and their life experiences (like values I hold that are important to me, might not be what others see as important and vice versa) but empathy and self-awareness, I feel these are more universal and it plays a much more significant role. Especially when 2 people don't see eye to eye, empathy and self-awareness from both parties can help defuse any tension.

I won't say there are any aspects about the core learning that are challenging (maybe the part forcing me to face my demons of self criticism). I think the challenging part is juggling to make sure that the core blogs are being written, whilst trying to find extra time to go over coding materials that I need more time on.

Yes, I must admit I do feel that I should be spending more time practicing programming instead of spending the time on core. Because it is hard enough being new to programming and learning all new things being crammed into a short time-frame, it is even harder when I know at the back of my mind that I need to complete this whole other part, that has nothing to do with programming. So I do end up spending less time looking in to the core stuff because I feel that learning programing should be my primary focus for now.

In saying all that, I can also see why a programming school would spend the time focusing on core learning in a web development course, because the human relations side of things are equally as important as learning how to code. No point being an expert coder who can't work well with anyone in any environment.
